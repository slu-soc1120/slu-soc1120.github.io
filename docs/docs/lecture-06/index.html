Lecture-06 - Social Structure

Division of Labor

This is a concept Émile Durkheim raised in his doctoral dissertation, in which he argued that capitalism had fundamentally altered how societies were organized. He described cohesion in pre-capitalist societies as mechanical solidarity, in which social ties bound more homogeneous groups of individuals together. With the advent of advanced capitalism, Durkheim argued that societies had shifted to organic solidarity, in which a division of labor bound more heterogeneous groups of people together. These ideas are similar to Ferdinand Tönnies’s ideas of Gemeinschaft and Gesellschaft, which had a significant impact on Max Weber’s thoughts on the division of labor. Gemeinschaft offers a parallel to Durkheim’s idea of mechanical solidarity, while Gesellschaft parallels organic solidarity.

People

We also briefly mentioned the following people:

  • Anthony Giddens (b. 1938) - English sociologist, who is now retired from the London School of Economics. Giddens remains a leading figure in contemporary sociological theory, well known for his critical re-readings of classical sociological theory.
  • Émile Durkheim (1858-1917) - French sociologist known for his work on mental health, religion, and the division of labor. In particular, we discussed his ideas about the division of labor this week.
  • Louis Althusser (1918-1990) - French philosopher known for his influence on neo-Marxist thought.
  • Max Weber (1864-1920) - German sociologist with wide-ranging early influence on the sociology of religion, economics, organizations, and politics
  • Talcott Parsons (1902-1979) - Sociologist best known for his influence on structural functionalist thought as well as his concept of the “sick role.” Today, we focused on the former.
